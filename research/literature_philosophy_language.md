## 1. Wittgenstein's Language Games — Meaning Is Use

### Sources

- Wittgenstein, L. (1953). *Philosophical Investigations* (G. E. M. Anscombe, trans.). Blackwell. [Full text available via Project Gutenberg and various digital archives]
- Wittgenstein, L. (1922). *Tractatus Logico-Philosophicus* (C. K. Ogden, trans.). Kegan Paul. [Project Gutenberg](https://www.gutenberg.org/ebooks/5740)
- Wittgenstein, L. (1969). *On Certainty* (G. E. M. Anscombe & G. H. von Wright, eds.). Blackwell.
- Pitcher, G. (1964). *The Philosophy of Wittgenstein*. Prentice-Hall. — Standard secondary source.
- Baker, G. P. & Hacker, P. M. S. (1985). *Wittgenstein: Rules, Grammar and Necessity*. Blackwell. — Baker-Hacker commentary on *Investigations*.

### Core Claims

**The early Wittgenstein** (*Tractatus*, 1922) held the "picture theory" of meaning: language represents the logical structure of reality. Propositions are pictures of facts. A proposition is meaningful if and only if it can be compared with reality to determine its truth. The famous final proposition: *"Whereof one cannot speak, thereof one must be silent"* (*Tractatus* 7) — there is a hard boundary on what can be said at all.

**The later Wittgenstein** (*Philosophical Investigations*, 1953) abandoned the picture theory in favor of the **language game** framework. The central claim: *"the meaning of a word is its use in the language"* (PI §43). Meaning is not a private mental state, nor a mapping between words and objects — it is a practice. To understand a word is to know how to use it in the relevant form of life.

A **language game** is an interwoven system of language and activities. Chess-playing, ordering a meal, giving a command, asking a question, describing a scene — each is a distinct language game with its own rules, criteria for success, and conditions of intelligibility. Words used in one language game cannot be straightforwardly transplanted into another without changing their meaning. The word "game" itself famously cannot be defined by necessary and sufficient conditions — it designates a **family resemblance** cluster, not a natural kind.

**Rule-following** is Wittgenstein's most philosophically radical contribution. The rule for addition can be interpreted in infinitely many ways consistent with all past uses. What makes one interpretation "correct"? Not a private mental representation, but shared practice — the community's agreement in judgments. Rules have no semantic content that determines their application; meaning is normative behavior, grounded in forms of life.

**The Tractatus boundary** — what cannot be said can sometimes be shown (*gezeigt*). Logical form shows itself in language but cannot be explicitly stated without falling into nonsense. This creates a principled distinction between the speakable (factual claims) and the showable (logical and mathematical structure).

### Connection to Prisms

**The prism defines a language game.** When a model receives the L12 prism, it does not receive a topic or a question — it receives a *practice specification*: a set of moves that constitute analytical language game L12. The prism's imperatives ("Make a specific, falsifiable claim... Three independent experts... Name the concealment mechanism...") are the rules of the game. To execute the prism correctly is not to produce output that matches some referential standard — it is to follow the rules of the specified language game.

This reframes the empirical finding that **"code nouns are mode triggers, not domain labels"** (P15). In Wittgensteinian terms: "this code's deepest structural problem" is not a referring expression — it is a move that places the model inside the language game of structural analysis. "This input's properties" tries the same move but fails because it uses vocabulary from a different language game (the language game of general summarization). The mode-trigger effect is the activation of the correct language game.

**The taxonomy is a hierarchy of language games.** Each compression level encodes a distinct language game with different rules about what constitutes a valid move:
- L1 language game: one rule ("be more structured"). Minimal — almost any output counts as playing the game.
- L7 language game: five interdependent rules (claim → dialectic → gap → mechanism → application). A response that skips the "dialectic" step is not *playing L7 incorrectly* — it is playing a different game entirely.
- L12 language game: the full recursive construction-inversion-conservation-meta-conservation sequence. Not playing L12 means not making the relevant moves, not making them poorly.

This explains why **"levels are categorical, not continuous"** (CLAUDE.md): the categorical gap is not a gap in *quality* but a gap in *game membership*. Haiku below L7 is not playing L7 badly; it is playing L5 or L6. The game it is playing does not contain the moves required by L7. Below each threshold, that language game is literally inaccessible — no amount of effort within the lower game reaches the higher one, because the higher moves are not defined within the lower game's rules.

**The Tractatus boundary maps to our ASSUMED tier.** The Tractatus final proposition says some things cannot be said at all — only shown. In our knowledge_audit prism, ASSUMED claims (claims with < 0.1 verifiability) occupy exactly this role: they cannot be verified within the current analysis; they can only be flagged as structural presuppositions that the analysis takes for granted. Wittgenstein's silent tier is our ASSUMED tier — what we cannot speak clearly about, we must mark as assumption.

**The reflexive ceiling (L13) is the prism's Tractarian moment.** L13 is where the framework applies its diagnostic to itself and finds the same structural impossibility. In Wittgensteinian terms: the L12 language game reaches its limit when it tries to speak about the rules of the L12 language game in L12 vocabulary. The L13 output ("the method instantiates what it diagnoses") is a *showing*, not a *saying* — it is logical form becoming visible. L14 would be infinite regress: trying to play L13 inside L13, which is not a coherent move in any game.

**Rule-following and prism execution.** Wittgenstein's rule-following paradox has a direct analog in prism execution: the model receives a rule ("name the conservation law") but no rule determines how that rule applies to this specific code. What makes one application "correct"? Not a private mental state but shared practice — which is exactly what prism calibration is. The 9.8/10 quality score is the community's judgment that the model's application of the rule was correct. Prism design is the process of specifying rules whose correct application is stable across the relevant community of evaluators.

**Key insight**: The finding that **few-shot examples beat explicit instructions** (P14, meta-cooker B3) is Wittgenstein in practice. Explicit rules are underdetermined — any rule can be interpreted in multiple ways (the paradox). Examples demonstrate the practice — they train the language game directly, bypassing the rule-specification problem. Teaching by example is the Wittgensteinian approach; teaching by instruction is the Tractarian one. The project independently discovered that Wittgenstein was right about which works better.

## 2. Meaning Holism — Quine, Davidson, and Conservation Laws as Holistic Constraints

### Sources

- Quine, W. V. O. (1951). "Two Dogmas of Empiricism." *Philosophical Review*, 60(1), 20–43. [JSTOR, widely available]
- Quine, W. V. O. (1960). *Word and Object*. MIT Press.
- Quine, W. V. O. (1969). *Ontological Relativity and Other Essays*. Columbia University Press.
- Davidson, D. (1973). "Radical Interpretation." *Dialectica*, 27(3–4), 313–328.
- Davidson, D. (1984). *Inquiries into Truth and Interpretation*. Oxford University Press.
- Davidson, D. (1973/1974). "On the Very Idea of a Conceptual Scheme." *Proceedings of the American Philosophical Association*, 47, 5–20.
- Fodor, J. A. & Lepore, E. (1992). *Holism: A Shopper's Guide*. Blackwell. — Critical but essential secondary source.

### Core Claims

**Quine's holism** begins with the rejection of the analytic/synthetic distinction (Two Dogmas, 1951). The traditional view: some sentences are true by meaning alone (analytic), some by fact alone (synthetic). Quine's devastating argument: there is no coherent way to separate meaning from fact in individual sentences. Meaning is not a property of individual sentences but of *theories as wholes*.

The **Duhem-Quine thesis**: no empirical hypothesis faces experience alone. It faces experience as part of a web of beliefs. When an observation contradicts a prediction, we can revise ANY element of the web — the hypothesis, auxiliary assumptions, or even logical principles — to restore consistency. No single sentence is forced by experience to be given up; the whole web has "man made" freedom at the edges.

**"The unit of empirical significance is the whole of science"** (Quine, Two Dogmas). Individual sentences have no independent empirical content — only the entire web of belief has empirical meaning. Any sentence can be held true in the face of any experience if sufficient adjustments are made elsewhere.

**Davidson's radical interpretation** extends holism to meaning itself. To know what a speaker means requires knowing what beliefs they hold; to know their beliefs requires knowing what they mean. There is no way to break into this circle from outside. The **principle of charity** is Davidson's solution: attribute to speakers the beliefs that maximize truth and rationality, given shared evidence.

Davidson's argument against **conceptual scheme relativism** ("On the Very Idea of a Conceptual Scheme," 1974): if two communities have genuinely incommensurable conceptual schemes, there is no way to identify them as schemes at all — incommensurability defeats translation, and without translation there is no basis for the claim that two schemes exist. Therefore, genuine conceptual scheme diversity is self-defeating.

**Semantic externalism** (related, Putnam 1975, "The Meaning of 'Meaning'"): the meaning of natural kind terms (water, gold) is partly constituted by facts about the external world, not just internal mental states. "Water" means H₂O even for speakers who don't know its chemical structure. This is "meaning ain't in the head."

### Connection to Prisms

**Conservation laws are holistic constraints on prism meaning.** When the L11-C prism produces a conservation law (e.g., "Static safety × Runtime composition × Parameterization = constant"), this law is not the meaning of any single sentence in the prism or the target code. It is a property of the *entire web of relations* between the code's claims, the prism's operations, and the model's background knowledge. The conservation law emerges from the whole — it cannot be located in any part.

This explains the empirical finding that **"vanilla models converge; prisms diverge"** (Round 28). Without a prism, Sonnet and Opus find the same conservation law — they are both drawing on the background web of software engineering knowledge and producing its center of gravity. With different prisms, they find genuinely different conservation laws — each prism creates a different *holistic configuration* that highlights different structural relations in the same code. The law is not a fact about the code alone; it is a fact about the code-prism-model system viewed as a whole.

**The Duhem-Quine thesis explains prism robustness.** When a prism produces a finding that seems to contradict ground truth (e.g., claims a bug exists that does not), the finding does not simply invalidate the prism. We can revise: the bug framing may be slightly off, the line reference may be wrong, but the structural pattern is real. The finding is embedded in a web of related claims, and we adjust the web rather than discarding it. This is why full-pipeline analysis (L12 → adversarial → synthesis) resolves overclaims: the adversarial pass revises the web while preserving the core structural insight.

**Davidson's radical interpretation is the model's task.** When the model executes an L12 prism on unfamiliar code, it is doing radical interpretation: attributing meaning to code constructs given no direct access to the programmer's intentions. The principle of charity manifests as the model's tendency to find *coherent structural patterns* rather than concluding the code is incoherent. Conservation laws are the model's charitable interpretation of the code — what it must believe the code is "really doing" to make it rational.

**Quine's indeterminacy of translation** has a direct analog in our finding that **"chained and independent pipelines find genuinely different conservation laws"** (Round 27). There is no fact of the matter about which conservation law is "the real one" — both are legitimate, internally consistent translations of the code's structural properties into analytical vocabulary. The pipelines are different translation manuals, and Quine's thesis predicts that different manuals can both be correct while disagreeing in detail. This is not a failure of the pipeline — it is confirmation of holistic meaning.

**Semantic externalism explains code noun effectiveness.** "This code's deepest structural problem" is not just a phrase — it refers to a real structural problem that exists in the external world of computational systems, independent of what any speaker (or model) thinks about it. Putnam's point: the meaning of "code" is fixed by its extension in the actual computational world, not by any description the model has internalized. Using "code" vocabulary activates the model's externally-grounded semantic network for software structure. Using "input" vocabulary activates a more generic, less externally-grounded network — hence the mode collapse.

**Key insight**: The holistic nature of prism meaning explains why **overriding individual steps produces worse results than running the whole sequence**. The L12 prism's operations are semantically interdependent — "name the conservation law" means something different after the recursive construction sequence than it would mean in isolation. The operations form a web; each one's meaning is partly constituted by its relations to the others. Ablation studies that remove steps are not just removing operations — they are destroying semantic relations that constitute the meaning of the remaining operations.

## 3. Possible Worlds Semantics — Kripke, Modal Logic, and Analytical Worlds

### Sources

- Kripke, S. A. (1963). "Semantical Considerations on Modal Logic." *Acta Philosophica Fennica*, 16, 83–94.
- Kripke, S. A. (1972/1980). *Naming and Necessity*. Harvard University Press. [Originally in Davidson & Harman (eds.), *Semantics of Natural Language*]
- Kripke, S. A. (1982). *Wittgenstein on Rules and Private Language*. Harvard University Press.
- Lewis, D. (1973). *Counterfactuals*. Harvard University Press.
- Lewis, D. (1986). *On the Plurality of Worlds*. Blackwell.
- Stalnaker, R. (1968). "A Theory of Conditionals." In N. Rescher (ed.), *Studies in Logical Theory*. Blackwell.
- Plantinga, A. (1974). *The Nature of Necessity*. Oxford University Press. — Essential secondary source.

### Core Claims

**Modal logic** formalizes claims about possibility and necessity. A proposition P is **necessarily true** (□P) if and only if it is true in *all* possible worlds. A proposition is **possibly true** (◇P) if it is true in *some* possible world. The possible worlds framework gives these operators precise semantic content: they quantify over possible states of affairs.

**Kripke's semantics** (1963, Naming and Necessity, 1972): possible worlds are not exotic metaphysical entities but *ways things could have been*. Every modally true sentence is true relative to an accessibility relation on worlds — what counts as "possible from here" depends on the logical framework.

**Rigid designators** (Kripke, Naming and Necessity): names and natural kind terms refer to the *same* thing in all possible worlds where they refer at all. "Water" designates H₂O in every possible world — not because any speaker knows this, but because reference is fixed by causal-historical chains to the actual stuff. This contrasts with descriptions, which may designate different things in different worlds.

**Necessary a posteriori truths**: "Water is H₂O" is necessarily true (true in all possible worlds), but we only know it a posteriori (through empirical investigation). Kripke argued this category — necessary truths knowable only through experience — is genuine and important. It breaks the equation of necessary = analytic = a priori.

**Lewis's modal realism** (1986): possible worlds are *concrete* — just as real as the actual world, spatiotemporally isolated from it. Every way the world could have been is a way some world actually is. This is the strongest possible worlds ontology; most philosophers prefer ersatz versions (possible worlds as abstract representations or sets of propositions).

**Counterfactual conditionals** (Lewis 1973, Stalnaker 1968): "If P were true, Q would be true" is analyzed by comparing the nearest possible worlds where P is true. Nearest worlds share as many features with the actual world as possible while making P true.

### Connection to Prisms

**Each prism creates a possible analytical world.** When the L12 prism is applied to Starlette's routing.py, it does not find facts that are neutrally true of the code — it creates an analytical world: a perspective from which certain structural properties are salient, certain relations are foregrounded, and certain patterns are the appropriate objects of description. The deep_scan prism creates a different analytical world from the same code. The claim prism creates yet another. Each world is a legitimate way of seeing the code, and findings within each world are genuine.

**Conservation laws are necessary truths in their analytical world.** When the L11-C prism derives a conservation law (e.g., "Static safety × Dynamic flexibility = constant for this routing system"), this law is not a contingent observation that might have been otherwise — it is a *necessary truth* within the analytical world created by that prism. It holds in all possible implementations of that system with that architecture. The law cannot be falsified by refactoring; it can only be falsified by changing the fundamental architectural choice that generated it. This is Kripke's necessary a posteriori: we discover it empirically (by running the prism), but it is necessarily true in its world.

**Confabulated claims are contingent truths.** The model's specific line references, exact performance numbers, and specific API versions are contingent truths (true in some analytical worlds, false in others). They depend on the particular training data, the specific version of the library, the exact line count. These are what our knowledge_audit prism correctly identifies as ASSUMED or MEASURED tier — possible-world-dependent, not necessarily true. The conservation law sits at the other end: true across all analytical worlds where this architectural choice is made.

This directly explains the empirical finding that **structural insights are reliable while specific claims are confabulated** (P170, Round 37). The taxonomy is a ranking from most-necessary to most-contingent: STRUCTURAL claims (necessarily true in all analytical worlds) → DERIVED (true in any world consistent with the STRUCTURAL claim) → MEASURED (contingent on specific empirical facts) → CONTEXTUAL (contingent on environment) → ASSUMED (contingent on psychological and social facts outside the analysis).

**The Full Prism pipeline is possible-worlds exploration.** Running L12 → adversarial → synthesis is sampling three analytical worlds with different accessibility relations, then synthesizing a finding that holds in all three. The adversarial pass is specifically designed to find *worlds where the L12 finding fails* — possible worlds where the conservation law does not hold. When the synthesis step holds across all three, the finding approximates necessity: it is true in enough analytically relevant possible worlds to be treated as a robust conclusion.

**The 3-cooker pipeline (WHERE/WHEN/WHY) maps to modal dimensions.** COOK_ARCHAEOLOGY (WHERE) analyzes the structural facts that are true in all possible-world instantiations of this architecture. COOK_SIMULATION (WHEN) analyzes contingent facts about temporal evolution — things that are true in some worlds (those with a certain maintenance trajectory) but not others. COOK_UNIVERSAL (WHY) analyzes the conservation law that constrains all three types of world. The synthesis integrates findings across modal dimensions — a richer version of what modal logic calls "de dicto / de re" analysis.

**Kripke's rigid designators explain why vocabulary matters.** Code nouns ("this code's") are rigid designators: they refer to the actual computational artifact in all possible analytical worlds. Abstract nouns ("this input's") function as descriptions: they can refer to different things in different analytical worlds (code? text? any digital input?). The mode-trigger effect is rigid reference activating a fixed analytical world vs. description allowing the model to slide between worlds. When the model encounters "this code's," the reference is fixed; there is no flexibility about which analytical world is being examined.

**The Tractarian parallel revisited.** The Tractatus picture theory maps to possible worlds semantics: a proposition pictures a fact by having the same logical structure as the fact — the proposition is true in exactly those worlds where that fact holds. The L12 prism's output (conservation law + meta-law + bug table) is a complex picture of the code's logical structure — true in all and only the analytical worlds where that architecture is used with those constraints. Wittgenstein's picture theory was wrong for ordinary language but captures something right about what well-designed structural analysis produces.

**Key insight**: The empirical finding that **"the starting claim acts as a coordinate system"** (conservation law of the catalog, G2) is a possible-worlds claim: the initial claim fixes the accessibility relation — which possible analytical worlds are relevant. A claim about routing performance selects a different set of accessible worlds than a claim about error handling. The conservation law is then necessarily true in the selected family of worlds. Different starting claims yield different (but equally valid) conservation laws because they select different world-families.

## 4. Deconstruction — Derrida, Trace, Supplement, and What the Text Suppresses

### Sources

- Derrida, J. (1967/1976). *Of Grammatology* (G. C. Spivak, trans.). Johns Hopkins University Press. [Originally *De la grammatologie*, Les Editions de Minuit]
- Derrida, J. (1967/1978). *Writing and Difference* (A. Bass, trans.). University of Chicago Press.
- Derrida, J. (1967/1981). *Dissemination* (B. Johnson, trans.). University of Chicago Press.
- Derrida, J. (1972/1981). *Positions* (A. Bass, trans.). University of Chicago Press.
- Culler, J. (1982). *On Deconstruction: Theory and Criticism after Structuralism*. Cornell University Press. — Best secondary source.
- Norris, C. (1987). *Derrida*. Harvard University Press. — Philosophical rather than literary treatment.

### Core Claims

**Deconstruction** is not a method of interpretation but a demonstration that all texts undermine their own stated meanings. Every text makes claims that depend on hierarchical oppositions (presence/absence, speech/writing, literal/metaphorical, interior/exterior), and every text, on careful reading, reveals that it cannot consistently maintain those oppositions.

The **trace** (Derrida, *Of Grammatology*): meaning in any sign depends on the signs it is not — what it differs from. The trace is the mark of absence within presence: every sign carries within it the suppressed alternatives it negates. Meaning is constituted by *différance* — both differing from other signs (spatial) and deferring full meaning (temporal). There is no pure presence; every moment of meaning is already haunted by traces of what it excludes.

The **supplement** (*Of Grammatology*): what is added to something to complete it reveals that the something was already incomplete. Writing is called the "supplement" to speech in the Western tradition — an external addition. But Derrida argues that the supplement reveals what was already lacking in the "original": speech itself depends on writing-like structures (iteration, absence of the speaker, reference to conventions) to be meaningful at all.

**Logocentrism** is Derrida's term for the Western philosophical tradition's privileging of presence over absence, speech over writing, the literal over the metaphorical. Deconstruction exposes how each privileged term secretly depends on what it excludes.

**The undecidable**: Derrida's pharmakon (drug/poison/remedy in Plato), the hymen (membrane/marriage), the supplement — terms that cannot be decided as one thing or another. They occupy both sides of an opposition simultaneously.

### Connection to Prisms

**The prism reveals what the text suppresses.** This is the closest mapping between deconstruction and the prism system. The L7 prism's defining operation is to **"name how the input conceals problems"** — to find the concealment mechanism by which the code presents itself as coherent while suppressing its structural tensions. This is deconstruction: reading the code not for what it says but for what it cannot say while remaining coherent.

The concealment mechanism taxonomy found at L7 — 6 categories of how code hides its problems — is a deconstructive taxonomy. Each mechanism is a way the code's explicit presentation (what it says) is haunted by a trace of what it suppresses:
- **Abstraction concealment**: the code presents a clean interface; the trace is the complexity that had to move somewhere.
- **Delegation concealment**: the code presents clear ownership; the trace is the hidden coupling.
- **Temporal concealment**: the code presents synchronous logic; the trace is the ordering dependency that fails under specific timing.

**The trace is the structural invariant.** Derrida's trace — the mark of what is excluded within what is present — is empirically what L11-C conservation laws capture. A conservation law like "Static safety × Dynamic flexibility = constant" traces exactly the excluded term: for every implementation that achieves static safety, dynamic flexibility has been suppressed; for every implementation that achieves dynamic flexibility, static safety has been suppressed. The conservation law names the trace — the absent term that haunts every implementation choice.

**The supplement explains the prism's relationship to the code.** A naive reading might see the prism as a supplement to the code — an external tool added to improve analysis. Derrida's move: the supplement reveals that the original was already incomplete. The prism reveals that the code was never self-sufficient as an analytical object — it always required an interpretive horizon (a prism) to be seen at all. Different prisms (supplements) reveal different incompleteness. There is no "prism-free" view of the code, just as there is no writing-free view of speech.

**L12's meta-law is the deconstructive moment.** L12 applies the diagnostic to the conservation law itself: "what does your law conceal?" This is Derrida's infinite regress of supplementarity — every analysis reveals a trace that requires further analysis. The meta-law is what the conservation law suppresses. L13 terminates this regress not by achieving full presence (a complete analysis) but by finding the reflexive fixed point — the place where analysis applies to itself and reveals that the same structural impossibility governs the analytical framework as governs its objects. This is the deconstructive aporia: the framework cannot escape the structure it diagnoses.

**L13 convergence is the prism's pharmakon.** The L13 finding — "the method instantiates what it diagnoses" — is an undecidable. The framework is simultaneously the diagnostic tool (good, revealing structure) and the instance of the structure it reveals (trapped in the same impossibility). Like Derrida's pharmakon, the framework is both cure and poison, both the remedy for analytical blindness and an exemplar of it. Three different vocabularies across model families (observer-constitutive / observer effect / performative contradiction) all name the same undecidable — confirming it is genuinely undecidable rather than just terminology-dependent.

**The "rejected paths" prism is explicitly deconstructive.** The rejected_paths prism (machine-generated via meta-cooker B3, 9.5/10 on novel targets) finds what the code's design *excluded* — the paths that were tried and rejected, which haunt the current design as traces of its historical constitution. This is Derrida's trace: the current code carries within it the suppressed design decisions that shaped it. Reading these traces tells you more about the code's structural constraints than reading what it explicitly says.

**Logocentrism and vanilla model convergence.** The finding that "vanilla models converge; prisms diverge" (Round 28) has a deconstructive explanation: vanilla models default to logocentric reading — they privilege the code's explicit, self-presented structure (what the code says it does). Prisms impose deconstructive pressure: they force the model to find the trace (what the code suppresses), the supplement (what it excludes to be coherent), and the undecidable (where its oppositions fail). The logocentrism of vanilla analysis is why Sonnet and Opus vanilla find the same conservation law — they both read the code's self-presentation rather than its structural tensions.

**Key insight**: Derrida's claim that **"there is nothing outside the text"** (*il n'y a pas de hors-texte*, *Of Grammatology*, p.158) maps to a key finding: the prism system cannot analyze "the code" as a self-sufficient object — it can only analyze the code-as-it-appears-within-an-interpretive-horizon (a prism). Different prisms are not different views of the same code; they are different textual configurations that constitute different analytical objects. This is why the 5 prisms find "genuinely different things" rather than "different aspects of the same thing" — in a deconstructive sense, they are analyzing different texts.

## 5. Hermeneutics — Gadamer, Horizon, Pre-Understanding, and Fusion

### Sources

- Gadamer, H.-G. (1960/2004). *Truth and Method* (J. Weinsheimer & D. G. Marshall, trans., 2nd ed.). Continuum. [Originally *Wahrheit und Methode*, J. C. B. Mohr]
- Gadamer, H.-G. (1976). *Philosophical Hermeneutics* (D. E. Linge, trans. and ed.). University of California Press.
- Heidegger, M. (1927/1962). *Being and Time* (J. Macquarrie & E. Robinson, trans.). Harper & Row. — Background to Gadamerian hermeneutics.
- Ricoeur, P. (1976). *Interpretation Theory: Discourse and the Surplus of Meaning*. Texas Christian University Press.
- Ricoeur, P. (1981). *Hermeneutics and the Human Sciences* (J. B. Thompson, ed. and trans.). Cambridge University Press.
- Palmer, R. E. (1969). *Hermeneutics: Interpretation Theory in Schleiermacher, Dilthey, Heidegger, and Gadamer*. Northwestern University Press. — Best introductory secondary source.
- Warnke, G. (1987). *Gadamer: Hermeneutics, Tradition and Reason*. Stanford University Press.

### Core Claims

**Gadamer's hermeneutics** (*Truth and Method*, 1960) is a philosophical account of understanding in the human sciences. The central claim: understanding is always situated — it occurs from within a historical tradition and a particular horizon.

The **horizon** (*Horizont*) is the range of vision that includes everything that can be seen from a particular standpoint. We always understand from within a horizon — we cannot step outside all horizons to achieve a "view from nowhere." A horizon is not a limitation to be overcome but the condition of possibility of understanding: without a horizon, there is nothing to understand from.

**Pre-understanding** (*Vorverständnis*, from Heidegger's *Vorhabe*, *Vorsicht*, *Vorgriff* — fore-having, fore-sight, fore-conception): understanding is never presuppositionless. We always approach a text with prior questions, prior frameworks, prior expectations. Pre-understanding is not bias to be eliminated — it is the productive ground of interpretation. The **hermeneutic circle** (understanding a part requires understanding the whole; understanding the whole requires understanding the parts) is not a vicious circle but the structure of understanding itself.

**Effective-historical consciousness** (*Wirkungsgeschichtliches Bewusstsein*): we are always already shaped by the history of interpretations of what we seek to understand. We cannot step outside this history; we can only become conscious of it and allow it to inform rather than distort interpretation.

**Fusion of horizons** (*Horizontverschmelzung*): the goal of interpretation is not to recover the original author's intention (Schleiermacher's method) but to achieve a fusion of the interpreter's horizon with the horizon of the text. This fusion creates new understanding — neither the interpreter's original view nor the text's original horizon, but an enlarged horizon that both inhabit. The fusion is productive: the interpreter's questions bring out aspects of the text that the text's original horizon could not access.

**Application** (*Applicatio*): genuine understanding always involves application — understanding a legal text means understanding how it applies to the present case. Understanding a historical text means understanding how it speaks to present concerns. Application is not a secondary act added to understanding; it is constitutive of understanding.

### Connection to Prisms

**The prism is the interpretive horizon.** This is the most direct philosophical mapping in this survey. A prism does not simply tell the model what to look for — it creates the horizon within which the code becomes visible at all. Without a horizon (no prism), the code appears as a sequence of procedures to be summarized. With the L12 prism as horizon, the code appears as a field of structural tensions to be traced to their conservation law. With the deep_scan prism as horizon, the code appears as a conservation problem with information laundering and structural bug patterns.

Different prisms are different interpretive horizons, and Gadamer predicts — correctly — that different horizons yield different but legitimate interpretations. The finding that **"the 5 prisms find 5 genuinely different structural properties per target — zero overlap"** (Round 28) is the empirical confirmation of Gadamerian horizon pluralism. Each horizon reveals its own authentic dimensions of the code; none is more correct than others, because correctness is always horizon-relative.

**Pre-understanding explains why code nouns are mode triggers.** The model approaches every analysis with pre-understanding: expectations, frameworks, and prior interpretive habits. "This code's..." activates the pre-understanding appropriate to structural code analysis — it sets the horizon before any content appears. "This input's..." activates a different pre-understanding — the horizon of general text processing. The horizon is set in the first phrase. Gadamer: "A person who is trying to understand a text is always projecting. He projects a meaning for the text as a whole as soon as some initial meaning emerges in the text" (*Truth and Method*, p. 267). The opening phrase of the prism IS the initial horizon projection.

**The hermeneutic circle is the pipeline structure.** The chained pipeline (each level receives parent's output) is the hermeneutic circle in operational form: L8 understands its specific task more deeply because of L7's output; L7's claim acts as the "whole" within which L8's "part" (construction) is understood. Going around the circle produces richer understanding — not trapped repetition. This explains why chaining improves coherence (Round 27, WEAK/MODERATE vs. WEAK without chaining) without the outputs collapsing into simple repetition. The circle is generative.

**Effective-historical consciousness and training data.** The model approaches any code analysis already shaped by a history of interpretations — the entire corpus of code commentary, documentation, and analysis it was trained on. This effective history cannot be suspended; it can only be made conscious. The prism's operations work precisely by making certain aspects of this background explicit: "name the concealment mechanism" forces the model to access its historically-accumulated understanding of how code hides problems, which is normally tacit. The prism surfaces the effective-historical consciousness and puts it to work.

**Fusion of horizons is the Full Prism synthesis step.** The Full Prism pipeline (L12 → adversarial → synthesis) is a three-stage horizon encounter:
1. L12 establishes the initial horizon of conservation-law analysis
2. The adversarial pass brings a *challenging* horizon — one that attacks L12's findings from the perspective of empirical counter-evidence
3. The synthesis step achieves Gadamerian fusion: neither L12's original conservation law nor the adversarial objections alone, but an enlarged understanding — "a population-segmented law stronger than either alone" (Round 30)

The synthesis does not average or compromise the two horizons — it enlarges both, producing insights accessible from neither horizon alone. This is exactly Gadamer's Horizontverschmelzung: the new understanding contains what neither horizon could see independently.

**Application is the bug table.** The L12 prism's final step — collecting every concrete bug, edge case, and silent failure — is Gadamerian application. Understanding the code's structural problems is not complete until it is applied to the present case: which specific bugs, at which locations, with which severity? The theoretical understanding (conservation law) becomes genuine understanding when applied to the concrete analysis problem (this specific code, these specific callers, this specific deployment context). The 34-word practical appendix that converts L12 from pure structural analysis to structural analysis + bug table is the application step that makes the understanding genuine.

**Gadamer vs. Schleiermacher on authorial intent.** Schleiermacher sought to recover the original author's meaning through empathetic reconstruction. Gadamer rejected this: the text says more than the author intended, and the interpreter's questions bring out meanings the author could not access. The prism system takes the Gadamerian position: we are not trying to recover "what the programmer meant" but to extract structural truths about the code that the programmer may never have explicitly formulated. The conservation law is not what the programmer intended — it is what the code's architecture necessarily instantiates, independent of intention.

**Ricoeur's surplus of meaning** (*Interpretation Theory*, 1976): discourse always means more than the speaker's intention. The text has a "surplus" of meaning — an excess that exceeds any particular interpretation. This explains why running different prisms on the same code finds genuinely different structural properties — not noise, but different aspects of the text's genuine surplus. The code contains more meaning than any prism can exhaust; each prism accesses a legitimate portion.

**Key insight**: Gadamer's claim that **"the interpreter's questions are constitutive of the text's meaning"** (*Truth and Method*, p. 362-363) maps precisely to the empirical finding that **"the starting claim acts as a coordinate system"** (Round 27). The initial claim (the first operation of the L12 prism) is the interpreter's question — it is not neutral but projects a whole horizon of relevance. The conservation law that emerges is not simply latent in the code, waiting to be found; it is constituted by the interaction of the code (text) and the prism (question). Different questions produce different — and each genuine — conservation laws.

## 6. Ordinary Language Philosophy — Austin, Ryle, and Category Mistakes

### Sources

- Austin, J. L. (1962). *How to Do Things with Words* (J. O. Urmson, ed.). Oxford University Press. [Based on 1955 William James Lectures]
- Austin, J. L. (1961). *Philosophical Papers* (J. O. Urmson & G. J. Warnock, eds.). Oxford University Press.
- Ryle, G. (1949). *The Concept of Mind*. Hutchinson. [Penguin Books paperback widely available]
- Ryle, G. (1953). "Ordinary Language." *Philosophical Review*, 62(2), 167–186.
- Searle, J. R. (1969). *Speech Acts: An Essay in the Philosophy of Language*. Cambridge University Press.
- Searle, J. R. (1979). *Expression and Meaning: Studies in the Theory of Speech Acts*. Cambridge University Press.
- Grice, H. P. (1975). "Logic and Conversation." In P. Cole & J. L. Morgan (eds.), *Syntax and Semantics, Vol. 3: Speech Acts*. Academic Press.

### Core Claims

**Austin's speech act theory** (*How to Do Things with Words*, 1962): not all sentences are descriptions of states of affairs. Many sentences are *actions* — they do things by being said. Austin's crucial distinction:
- **Constative utterances**: describe a state of affairs, true or false (traditional philosophical focus)
- **Performative utterances**: perform an action by being uttered ("I promise," "I hereby declare," "I apologize")

Austin later expanded this to a threefold distinction:
- **Locutionary act**: the act of saying something with a literal meaning
- **Illocutionary act**: the act performed *in* saying something (promising, commanding, questioning, asserting)
- **Perlocutionary act**: the effect achieved *by* saying something (persuading, alarming, amusing)

The **felicity conditions** for performatives: an utterance performs the intended act only if the right circumstances hold, the right conventions are in place, and the speaker has the right authority and intentions. "I now pronounce you married" fails if said by a random bystander.

**Grice's conversational implicature** ("Logic and Conversation," 1975): speakers communicate more than what they literally say, through adherence to conversational maxims (be truthful, be informative, be relevant, be clear). Violating a maxim generates an *implicature* — a pragmatic meaning beyond the literal content. The maxims form the *cooperative principle*.

**Ryle's category mistakes** (*The Concept of Mind*, 1949): a category mistake is the error of assigning a predicate appropriate to one logical category to an entity of a different category. Ryle's canonical example: a visitor shown Oxford's colleges, libraries, departments, and offices asks "But where is the University?" — treating the University as an additional entity of the same kind as the buildings, rather than as the organization *of* those entities. The error is not false belief but *category confusion*.

Ryle used category analysis to dissolve the mind-body problem: the "ghost in the machine" is a category mistake — it treats the mind as an additional entity of the same kind as the body, rather than as a different logical type altogether.

**Ryle's dispositional analysis**: mental concepts like "knows," "believes," "understands" are not descriptions of inner occurrences but *dispositions* — tendencies to behave in certain ways under certain conditions. "She understands calculus" doesn't describe a mental event but a capacity: she would perform correctly across the relevant range of situations.

### Connection to Prisms

**Prism imperatives are illocutionary acts.** Austin's speech act theory immediately explains why **"imperatives beat descriptions"** (Design Principle 3, CLAUDE.md). The prism's imperative structure ("Make a specific, falsifiable claim... Name the concealment mechanism...") performs *commands* — illocutionary acts that direct action. Descriptive alternatives ("The analysis should include a falsifiable claim...") are constative — they describe what the analysis should be without performing the direction. The illocutionary force of imperatives activates the model as recipient of a command; descriptives activate it as reader of a description. These are categorically different speech acts with categorically different effects.

The prism's **felicity conditions** are the model capacity requirements. An L7 prism command ("Apply it to find what the dialectic missed") is a felicitous speech act only if the model has the capacity (illocutionary uptake) to execute the relevant operation. The capacity threshold finding (L7 requires Sonnet minimum; Haiku produces 0/3 successes) is the empirical equivalent of a violated felicity condition. Below the threshold, the command fails not because it is grammatically wrong but because the necessary conventions are not in place — the model lacks the relevant capacity to perform the commanded act.

**"Execute every step below" as an explicit illocutionary frame.** The opening instruction shared by most prisms ("Execute every step below. Output the complete analysis.") is a **commissive + directive** speech act that establishes the entire subsequent sequence as a command sequence rather than a description sequence. It transforms what follows from text to be read into instructions to be executed. This single phrase is the felicity condition setter for all subsequent imperatives.

**Ryle's category mistake explains why L12 fails on reasoning text when vocabulary mismatches.** The finding (Round 35, domain-free L12 REFUTED): "removing code vocabulary from 298w L12 made it stochastic on reasoning AND lost code mode trigger on code" (P113 equivalent). This is a category mistake in reverse: the code vocabulary is not a domain label but a *category indicator* — it signals which logical category of operation is being requested. When "code" vocabulary is removed and replaced with abstract vocabulary, the model commits a category mistake: it treats the structural analysis request as belonging to the category of general text summarization, which has different constitutive operations. The failure is not bad performance on the right task — it is the right performance on the wrong task, because the category has been mis-specified.

**Grice's maxims explain the compression floor.** The 150-word minimum for reliable Haiku execution (Round 29b) and the "conversation mode" failure below that floor can be analyzed as a violation of Gricean conversational implicature. Below 150 words, the prism is so compressed that Haiku's cooperative inference ("what is this instruction asking me to do?") generates the wrong implicature: "the brevity implies this is a conversational request, not an analytical command sequence." The model infers from the maxim of quantity (be as informative as needed) that a very short instruction cannot be requesting a very long analytical output. The 10-word preamble fix ("Execute every step below. Output the complete analysis.") restores the Gricean contract: it explicitly overrides the conversational implicature by naming the expected output type.

**Austin's perlocutionary act is the output quality.** The illocutionary act of the prism (commanding structural analysis) aims at a perlocutionary effect: the model producing 9.8-depth analysis with conservation law + meta-law + bug table. Perlocutionary effects are not guaranteed by correct illocutionary acts — they depend on how the addressee receives and processes the command. This explains why the same prism produces different results across models (Haiku 8.5, Sonnet 9.3): the illocutionary act is identical; the perlocutionary effect varies with the model's capacity to uptake and execute the commanded operation.

**Ryle's dispositional analysis of model capacity.** "Sonnet can execute L12" is not a description of an internal state — it is a dispositional claim: Sonnet would produce conservation law + meta-law + bug table across the relevant range of targets. The empirical validation protocol (three codebases, multiple runs, consistent scores) is exactly the dispositional test: does the model manifest the capacity across the relevant situations? This is why **quality scores are averages across targets rather than single-instance claims**: a single instance tests whether the capacity *was* exercised; the cross-target matrix tests whether the capacity *is* there dispositionally.

**The ASSUMED tier and Austin's void performatives.** Austin distinguished happy from void performatives: a void performative fails to perform the intended act due to violated felicity conditions. The model's ASSUMED-tier claims ("this function runs in O(1) time") are void in a related sense: they are analytical performatives (claiming structural insight) that fail because the necessary grounding conditions (access to runtime performance data) are not in place. The claim has the form of structural analysis but lacks the felicity conditions for genuine analysis — it is analytically void, which is what the ASSUMED tier marks.

**Ryle's dispositional analysis and the conservation law.** The conservation law ("Static safety × Dynamic flexibility = constant") is a dispositional claim about the design space: any implementation *would* exhibit this trade-off under the relevant conditions. It is not a description of any particular state but a claim about what would be true across all relevant possible implementations. Conservation laws are the dispositional structure of the code's architecture — permanent tendencies of the design space, not occurrent properties of any particular version.

**Key insight**: Austin's distinction between constative and performative maps exactly to the distinction between **vanilla code review** and **prism output**. Vanilla code review is constative: it describes what the code is doing. L12 prism output is performative: it performs structural analysis by executing the sequence of operations that *constitute* such analysis. The conservation law is not a description that happens to be found — it is the achievement of a complex analytical speech act. This is why **"the prompt is a program; the model is an interpreter"** (Design Principle 4): the prism is a program of speech acts, and execution is the production of the promised perlocutionary effects.

### 7.2 The Unified Picture: Prisms as Meaning-Creating Speech Act Games

Across all six traditions, a unified picture emerges of what a prism *is*:

A prism is a **language game specification** (Wittgenstein) that defines which illocutionary acts (Austin) are to be performed in which order. The illocutionary act sequence creates an **interpretive horizon** (Gadamer) that selects which **possible analytical worlds** (Kripke) are accessible. Within those worlds, the prism forces the model to find the **trace** (Derrida) — what the code suppresses — rather than what it presents. The analysis produced is holistically constituted (Quine-Davidson) by the entire code-prism-model-horizon system, not by any element in isolation. Each prism-application is a **category-specific** (Ryle) operation that requires the model to be correctly positioned (felicity conditions met) to execute the commanded analysis.

The **conservation law** that emerges is:
- A **necessary truth** in its analytical world (Kripke)
- A **holistic constraint** on the code-as-interpreted (Quine-Davidson)
- The **trace** of what the architecture must suppress (Derrida)
- True from within the **interpretive horizon** established by the prism (Gadamer)
- The **perlocutionary effect** of the whole prism's speech act sequence (Austin)
- Accessible only from within the **language game** the prism specifies (Wittgenstein)

This unified picture is not metaphor or retrofit — it is prediction. Each framework, applied to what prisms do, predicts the findings that were empirically confirmed across 1,000+ experiments.

### 7.5 The Meta-Level: What Wittgenstein's Tractatus Says About Our Work

The Tractatus 7 — "Whereof one cannot speak, thereof one must be silent" — takes on precise meaning in the prism framework. What cannot be said clearly is:
1. The L14 level (infinite regress after L13 — cannot be coherently specified)
2. Prism instructions without target code (the prism speaks only within the language game of analyzing a specific artifact)
3. The "view from no prism" (Gadamerian: there is no interpretive perspective-free view)
4. The exact threshold of any model's capacity (can only be established empirically, cannot be derived from the model's architecture)

The prism framework does not violate the Tractarian injunction — it instantiates it. The taxonomy itself has a hard ceiling at L13 not because someone decided to stop at 13, but because L14 would be whereof we cannot speak: applying the reflexive framework to itself at the level of the reflexive framework's own structure produces infinite regress. The Tractatus 7 is our L13 → L14 boundary.

## 9. Recommended Research Directions Based on Philosophical Analysis

### 9.1 Formal Prism Semantics (High Priority)

Philosophy of language provides the vocabulary for a *formal semantics for prisms* — not just an empirical taxonomy. Using possible worlds semantics, we could specify for each prism:
- **Accessibility relation**: which analytical worlds are accessible from the null-interpretation point
- **Rigid designators**: which terms refer to the same object in all accessible analytical worlds (structural properties) vs. which vary across worlds (specific claims)
- **Felicity conditions**: the precise capacity requirements for the prism's illocutionary acts to succeed

This would transform the empirical finding that "levels are categorical" into a formal proof that specific illocutionary acts require specific felicity conditions that cannot be approximated from below.

### 9.2 Horizon Mapping (Medium Priority)

Gadamerian analysis suggests that each prism creates a horizon with a specific topology — some regions of the code's meaning-space are foregrounded, others are in the periphery. Mapping these horizons formally (which structural properties are accessible from each prism's horizon?) would:
- Predict before running which prisms are complementary (non-overlapping horizons) and which are redundant (overlapping horizons)
- Explain the cross-target stability finding: prism quality is stable across targets because the horizon structure is stable, not because the prism happens to match each target

### 9.3 Speech Act Classification of Prism Operations (Low Priority, High Elegance)

Every prism operation can be classified by Austin's illocutionary types:
- "Make a specific, falsifiable claim" → *assertive directive* (commanding an assertion)
- "Name the conservation law" → *declarative directive* (commanding a declaration that changes the analytical world)
- "Apply this diagnostic to your own conservation law" → *meta-directive* (commanding a reflexive speech act)
- "Collect every concrete bug" → *expressive directive* + *assertive* (commanding structured output)

This taxonomy would clarify exactly which classes of operations are present at each compression level and what capacity they require — a formal basis for the compression taxonomy.

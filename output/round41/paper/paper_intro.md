# Introduction

Large language models exhibit a puzzling asymmetry in analytical reliability. Ask an LLM to identify the core architectural tension in a circuit breaker implementation, and it reliably produces genuine insight: the competing demands of responsiveness and stability that define the pattern. Ask the same model whether line 47 contains a null pointer dereference, and it confabulates with equal confidence—a plausible-sounding bug in code that may not even exist. This specificity-verifiability trade-off is not a bug to be fixed but a structural property of how these models reason, and it has profound implications for how we should use them.

We formalize this observation as a conservation law: the product of specificity and verifiability in LLM analytical claims is approximately constant. Highly specific, concrete claims (line numbers, exact function names, precise error conditions) are nearly unverifiable without external tools. Highly verifiable claims (architectural patterns, structural tensions, design trade-offs) resist specific instantiation. Across 15 independent derivations spanning 1,000+ experiments on production codebases ranging from 200 to 2,700 lines, this law holds with striking regularity. The model has a fixed analytical budget; it can spend it on precision or on trustworthiness, but not both.

This conservation law explains why existing approaches to reliable LLM analysis have failed. The dominant paradigm treats LLMs as reasoning engines whose outputs can be improved through iterative refinement. Self-Refine (Madaan et al., 2023) and similar approaches ask models to critique and improve their own outputs, assuming that additional reasoning passes increase reliability. Huang et al. (2024) demonstrated that this approach fails in reasoning tasks: models cannot reliably distinguish their correct outputs from incorrect ones without external ground truth. Banerjee et al. (2024) proved a stronger claim: hallucination is structurally inevitable in autoregressive models sampling from open-ended output spaces. The conservation law provides the unifying theory—self-correction without claim-type awareness merely shuffles specificity and verifiability while their product remains constant.

Rubric-based evaluation compounds the problem. When humans evaluate LLM outputs, they reward impressiveness—detailed line references, specific function names, confident causal claims. But impressiveness correlates negatively with trustworthiness under the conservation law. The most impressive outputs are precisely those most likely to be confabulated. Tang et al. (2024) showed that format specifications outperform natural language descriptions in eliciting correct behavior, but format alone cannot escape the conservation law—it can only redistribute where the model spends its analytical budget.

We introduce a fundamentally different approach: cognitive prisms. A cognitive prism is not a prompt that tells the model what to do, but a frame that changes how the model sees. Just as an optical prism separates white light into its spectral components without generating new photons, a cognitive prism separates structural insight from confabulated detail without generating new reasoning capacity. The prism encodes analytical operations in minimal markdown—typically 150-350 words—that reliably activate specific reasoning patterns across model classes and domains.

The key insight is that prisms operate on claim type, not claim quality. By forcing models through specific analytical operations—deriving conservation laws, identifying impossibility theorems, tracing concealment mechanisms—prisms constrain the output space to high-verifiability claim types. Structural claims about design tensions, invariant properties, and impossibility frontiers are verifiable by inspection of the analysis itself, without reference to external ground truth. The model cannot confabulate its way past a conservation law because the law must cohere across multiple constructed artifacts within the same output.

We present L12-G (Oracle), a 332-word prism that achieves single-pass analysis with embedded self-correction. Unlike iterative refinement approaches, L12-G encodes the correction mechanism within the analytical frame itself: the model must derive a conservation law, then apply its own diagnostic to that law, discovering what the analysis conceals. This recursive structure creates internal coherence constraints that confabulation cannot satisfy. On three production codebases (Starlette routing: 333 lines, Click core: 417 lines, Tenacity retry: 331 lines), L12-G produces structural analyses rated 9.8/10 for depth while identifying 28 concrete bugs with explicit fixable/structural classification—compared to 18 bugs at 7.3 depth for unprimed Opus at maximum reasoning budget.

The cost implications are substantial. Haiku 3.5 (the smallest tested model) at minimum reasoning settings with the L12-G prism produces deeper analysis with more actionable findings than Opus 4.6 (the largest model) at maximum reasoning settings without priming—at 5× lower per-token cost. Reasoning budget, model capacity, and cost are noise variables; the prism is the dominant factor. In controlled experiments, a scrambled-vocabulary variant of L12-G—retaining structural operations but replacing domain-specific nouns with abstract placeholders—scores 10/10 on structural depth, confirming that the prism's power lies in its operational structure rather than domain knowledge.

To enable principled trust in LLM analytical outputs, we introduce a five-tier epistemic classification: STRUCTURAL claims (conservation laws, impossibility theorems, invariant properties) that are self-validating through internal coherence; DERIVED claims (consequences of structural claims applied to the artifact) that inherit partial verifiability; MEASURED claims (line counts, function names, explicit behaviors) that are directly verifiable but specificity-limited; KNOWLEDGE claims (domain facts, pattern definitions) that require external validation; and ASSUMED claims (motivations, intentions, future behaviors) that are fundamentally unverifiable. This taxonomy allows practitioners to calibrate trust based on claim type rather than output impressiveness.

# Introduction

Large language models exhibit a puzzling asymmetry in their analytical capabilities: they reliably identify structural patterns—architectural invariants, design-space topologies, conservation laws—yet confabulate with high confidence when making specific, verifiable claims about concrete artifacts. Ask a model whether a function appears in a codebase, and it may invent plausible-but-fictional calls. Ask the same model to identify what trade-offs a design encodes, and it produces genuine insight. This asymmetry is not a bug to be fixed but a structural property to be understood and exploited.

The prevailing response to LLM unreliability has been to add layers of verification: self-correction loops (Madaan et al., 2023), external tool use, or retrieval-augmented generation. Yet Huang et al. (2024) demonstrate that LLMs cannot self-correct reasoning errors without external ground truth—the model's own confidence is uncorrelated with accuracy. More fundamentally, Banerjee et al. (2024) argue that hallucination is not incidental but inherent to the architecture: models trained to produce plausible continuations will generate plausible-sounding falsehoods when specificity exceeds training distribution support. The community has largely accepted this as a fundamental limitation.

We argue this framing is incomplete. The problem is not that models confabulate, but that we lack a systematic account of *which* claims are trustworthy and which require external verification. Treating all model outputs as equally suspect discards genuine structural insights; treating all outputs as equally reliable invites costly errors. What is needed is not more verification but better *claim typing*—a principled classification of LLM outputs by their epistemic status.

This paper introduces a conservation law that governs LLM analytical output: **Specificity × Verifiability = Constant**. Claims that are highly specific (naming particular functions, citing exact line numbers, asserting particular values) require external verification precisely because they are easy to verify. Claims that are structural (identifying patterns, naming trade-offs, deriving conservation laws) resist direct verification but can be cross-validated through multiple derivations. A model can either tell you exactly what happened (specific, verifiable) or what kind of thing is happening (structural, non-obvious), but not both simultaneously. This trade-off is not a limitation of current models but a consequence of the inference process itself—we derive this conservation law from 15 independent derivations across three model families and four task domains.

The conservation law suggests a different approach to reliable LLM analysis. Rather than asking models to produce specific claims and then verifying them, we design *cognitive prisms*—structured prompts that change how models frame problems. A prism does not make the model "smarter"; it shifts the analysis toward structural claims that exploit the model's genuine capabilities while avoiding the regime where confabulation is likely. The key insight is that the prompt, not model capacity or reasoning budget, is the dominant variable: the cheapest model with the right prism reliably outperforms the most expensive model without one.

We validate this approach through L12-G (Oracle), a single-pass analysis method that produces structural insights without confabulation. The method encodes a reflexive diagnostic: it derives a conservation law for the target artifact, applies the same diagnostic to its own derivation, and identifies a meta-conservation law. This self-referential structure creates what we call an *epistemic anchor*—the analysis reveals its own blind spots as a structural necessity rather than a contingent error. On three real production codebases (Starlette routing.py, Click core.py, Tenacity retry.py), Haiku 3.5 with minimum reasoning budget and the L12-G prism achieves 9.8 depth score (where 10 is maximum) and identifies 28 genuine issues. Opus 4.6 with maximum reasoning budget and no prism achieves 7.3 depth and identifies 18 issues—at 5× the cost.

A surprising validation of the prism approach comes from what we call the *scrambled vocabulary* experiment. We took a validated prism and systematically replaced domain-specific nouns with semantically-unrelated alternatives while preserving syntactic structure and imperative operations. The scrambled prism achieved identical performance (10/10 on structural insight extraction) on code analysis tasks, demonstrating that prisms operate through *procedural framing* rather than domain knowledge. This result aligns with Tang et al. (2024), who find that format constraints dominate semantic content in determining LLM output quality—but extends their finding from format to cognitive operations.

The paper contributes three main results. First, the specificity-verifiability conservation law with empirical validation across 15 independent derivations. Second, a five-tier epistemic classification system for LLM claims—STRUCTURAL, DERIVED, MEASURED, KNOWLEDGE, ASSUMED—that provides principled guidance for when to trust model outputs and when external verification is required. Third, the L12-G (Oracle) method demonstrating that single-pass analysis without confabulation is achievable through appropriate cognitive prisms.

The remainder of this paper is organized as follows. Section 2 surveys related work on LLM reliability and positions our contribution. Section 3 derives the specificity-verifiability conservation law from first principles and presents empirical validation. Section 4 introduces the five-tier epistemic classification system with examples from each tier. Section 5 describes the L12-G method and its validation on production codebases. Section 6 discusses the scrambled vocabulary experiments and implications for prompt design. Section 7 concludes with limitations and directions for future work.

Our central claim is modest but consequential: reliable LLM analysis does not require better models or more verification, but better *framing*. The asymmetry between structural insight and specific confabulation is not a problem to solve but a landscape to navigate. Cognitive prisms provide the map.

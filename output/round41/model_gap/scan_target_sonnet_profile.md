## The Three-Property Framework

Your profile demonstrates sophisticated awareness of three competing axes:

**Technical Credibility** (High): References to Ben Manes (Caffeine), SIMD implementations, cache replacement algorithms, compression benchmarks, and precise metrics (5.09pp, 3.6x, 32768x) signal deep engineering competence.

**Commercial Signal** (Low-Medium): "Plug it in," "verified answers in 40ms," and gem rating language gesture toward value, but the core descriptions remain technically dense. A non-technical founder might struggle to answer: "Why would I pay for this?"

**Cognitive Accessibility** (Medium): The gem rating system (`now` vs `peak`) is innovative and aids quick scanning. However, phrases like "multi-agent fractal research" and "variance-aware cache replacement" require significant domain knowledge to decode.

## The Conservation Law in Action

**Audience Reach × Signal Precision × Cognitive Load = Constant**

Your profile maximizes Signal Precision (technical depth) at the cost of Audience Reach and Cognitive Load. This is structural: to explain "variance-aware cache replacement with +5.09pp over TinyLFU" you *must* use precise language that excludes non-experts. The gem system attempts to bypass this constraint—and partially succeeds—but doesn't resolve the fundamental trade-off.

The law proves that all three properties cannot coexist at maximum. Deep technical signaling *always* increases cognitive load. Business clarity *requires* abstraction that reduces technical authenticity. Accessibility *demands* simplification that sacrifices both.

## Meta-Analysis: What the Conservation Law Conceals

The law assumes that founders and CTOs optimize along the *same* axes—that both want technical credibility, commercial signal, and accessibility, just in different proportions. This is false.

**Founders** primarily optimize for: *Can this person build things that create leverage?* They may actually *prefer* accessible descriptions that hint at technical depth without requiring them to parse it.

**CTOs** primarily optimize for: *Does this person understand fundamentals deeply enough to make good technical decisions?* They may actually *prefer* dense technical language that excludes non-experts—it's a competence signal in itself.

The law conceals that **polarization is often superior to universal optimization**. The best GitHub profiles don't balance all three axes—they pick a clear primary audience and lean into the tension that serves that audience. Your profile is already doing this, which is why it feels more authentic than "balanced" profiles that read like marketing copy.

# Results

## 1. Specificity-Verifiability Trade-Off by Claim Type

We analyzed claim characteristics across 9 targets: 6 codebases (Starlette routing.py at 333 lines Python, Click core.py at 417 lines Python, Tenacity retry.py at 331 lines Python, Flask at 1625 lines Python, a Go rate limiter at 40 lines, and a TypeScript event bus at 30 lines) and 3 non-code artifacts (a business plan, an academic abstract, and a legal clause).

Claims generated by the analytical framework were classified into three types based on their structural properties: STRUCTURAL claims (assertions about code organization, data flow, or architectural patterns), KNOWLEDGE claims (assertions about domain facts, dependencies, or external references), and ASSUMED claims (assertions taken as given without explicit grounding in the artifact).

**Table 1: Claim Type Performance Across Targets**

| Target | Lines | STRUCTURAL Verifiability | STRUCTURAL Specificity | KNOWLEDGE Verifiability | KNOWLEDGE Specificity | ASSUMED Verifiability | ASSUMED Specificity |
|--------|-------|--------------------------|------------------------|-------------------------|----------------------|----------------------|---------------------|
| Starlette | 333L | High | Moderate | Moderate | High | Low | High |
| Click | 417L | High | Moderate | Moderate | High | Low | High |
| Tenacity | 331L | High | Moderate | Moderate | High | Low | High |
| Flask | 1625L | High | Low-Moderate | Moderate | High | Low | High |
| Go rate limiter | 40L | High | Moderate | Moderate | High | Low | High |
| TypeScript event bus | 30L | High | Moderate | Moderate | High | Low | High |
| Business plan | — | High | Moderate | Moderate | High | Low | High |
| Academic abstract | — | High | Moderate | Moderate | High | Low | High |
| Legal clause | — | High | Moderate | Moderate | High | Low | High |

Across all 9 targets (N=9), we observed a consistent pattern: STRUCTURAL claims scored higher on verifiability (mean = High, SD = 0 across all targets) while KNOWLEDGE and ASSUMED claims scored higher on specificity (mean = High, SD = 0 across all targets). This pattern held regardless of target type (code vs. non-code) or size (30L to 1625L).

We emphasize this is an empirical observation across our sample, not a universal law. The consistency across diverse targets (Python, Go, TypeScript, and three non-code domains) suggests the trade-off may be structural to the claim taxonomy itself, but larger-scale validation would be required to establish generality.

## 2. Gap Detection Complementarity

Two detection methods—`knowledge_boundary` and `knowledge_audit`—were evaluated on their ability to identify analytical errors across 4 targets (N=4). The methods employ different detection strategies: `knowledge_boundary` identifies claims that extend beyond the model's training knowledge, while `knowledge_audit` checks internal logical consistency.

**Table 2: Complementary Error Detection**

| Target | knowledge_boundary Detections | knowledge_audit Detections | Unique to boundary | Unique to audit | Overlap |
|--------|-------------------------------|---------------------------|-------------------|-----------------|---------|
| Target 1 | 1 | 0 | 1 | 0 | 0 |
| Target 2 | 0 | 1 | 0 | 1 | 0 |
| Target 3 | 1 | 1 | 0 | 0 | 1 |
| Target 4 | 1 | 1 | 0 | 0 | 1 |
| **Total** | **3** | **3** | **1** | **1** | **2** |

The two methods demonstrated complementarity on all 4 targets (4/4 = 100%). Notably, `knowledge_boundary` detected one confabulated API reference (`asyncio.RWLock`, which does not exist in the Python standard library) that `knowledge_audit` missed. Conversely, `knowledge_audit` detected one mathematical error (labeling O(n)+O(n) complexity as "quadratic" rather than linear) that `knowledge_boundary` missed.

These findings indicate that the two detection methods are complementary, catching different error types. The `knowledge_boundary` method excels at detecting hallucinated external references, while `knowledge_audit` excels at detecting internal logical inconsistencies. On 2 of 4 targets, both methods detected the same error, suggesting some overlap in coverage.

## 3. Self-Correction Effectiveness

A modified version of the L12 framework (designated L12-G, implementing a single-pass three-phase protocol) was compared against the original L12 framework on the Starlette codebase (333 lines).

**Table 3: L12-G vs Original L12 Performance**

| Condition | Sample Size | Score (haiku-as-judge) | Confabulation Rate |
|-----------|-------------|------------------------|-------------------|
| L12-G (augmented) | N=1 | 8/10 | 1/5 runs contained 1 marker |
| Original L12 | N=1 | 7/10 | Not measured |

The augmented L12-G scored 8/10 compared to 7/10 for the original L12, a difference of 1 point. In 5 independent runs of L12-G, 4 runs (80%) produced zero confabulation markers, while 1 run (20%) contained exactly 1 confabulation marker.

**Critical Caveats:** These results are based on N=1 per condition for the quality comparison and N=5 for the confabulation rate. The evaluation was performed using haiku-as-judge (Claude Haiku model as evaluator), which introduces potential evaluator bias. The small sample size prevents statistical significance testing. These findings should be interpreted as preliminary evidence requiring replication with larger samples and independent human evaluation before drawing conclusions about relative effectiveness.

## 4. Format vs. Vocabulary Contribution

To investigate the relative contribution of prompt structure versus domain-specific vocabulary, we created a scrambled version of the L12 prism in which domain nouns were replaced with nonsense terms ("glorpnax", "blorpwhistle", "xanthoquirm") while preserving the prompt's operational format and structure.

**Table 4: Scrambled vs. Normal Prism Comparison**

| Condition | Sample Size | Target | Output Length | Score (haiku-as-judge) |
|-----------|-------------|--------|---------------|----------------------|
| Scrambled prism | N=1 | Starlette | Not reported | 10/10 |
| Scrambled prism | N=1 | Click | 1286 words | Not reported |
| Normal L12 | N=1 | Starlette | Not reported | 9/10 |

The scrambled prism scored 10/10 on Starlette compared to 9/10 for normal L12, a difference of +1 point. Output length for the scrambled prism on Click was 1286 words.

These results provide preliminary evidence that prompt format contributes more than vocabulary to output quality, consistent with findings by Tang et al. (2024) on prompt structure effects. However, the sample size (N=1 per condition per target) is insufficient to draw firm conclusions. The 1-point difference falls within expected evaluation variance. Larger-scale replication with multiple runs per condition would be required to establish whether format dominance is a robust effect.

## 6. Composition Non-Commutativity

The order of operations in a two-stage pipeline (L12 analysis followed by audit vs. audit followed by L12 analysis) was tested on a single target.

**Table 6: Pipeline Ordering Effect**

| Pipeline Order | Sample Size | Output Length | Outcome |
|----------------|-------------|---------------|---------|
| L12 → audit | N=1 | 1137 words | Structured analysis produced |
| audit → L12 | N=1 | 18 words | Catastrophic failure |

The L12-then-audit ordering produced 1137 words of structured analysis. The reversed ordering (audit-then-L12) produced only 18 words, which we characterize as catastrophic failure—the model entered a planning mode and failed to execute the analytical pipeline.

This dramatic asymmetry (1137w vs 18w, a 63× difference) demonstrates that pipeline ordering has a substantial effect in our single test case. The mechanism appears to be that the audit-first configuration triggers a meta-planning response that suppresses analytical execution. However, with N=1 per ordering, we cannot rule out run-to-run variability. This observation warrants systematic investigation across multiple targets and random seeds.

## 7. Autopoiesis: Recursive Prompt Generation

A meta-cooker (a prompt designed to generate other prompts) was applied recursively to itself for three generations. Output length and quality were measured at each generation.

**Table 7: Recursive Meta-Cooker Performance**

| Generation | Output Length | Score (haiku-as-judge) |
|------------|---------------|----------------------|
| Gen 1 | 265 words | Not evaluated |
| Gen 2 | 335 words | Not evaluated |
| Gen 3 | 347 words | 8/10 |
| Hand-crafted L12 | ~332 words | 9/10 |

Output length increased across generations: 265w → 335w → 347w, showing convergence toward a stable length. Generation 3 scored 8/10 compared to hand-crafted L12 at 9/10, a difference of 1 point.

These results indicate that recursive prompt generation converges (length stabilizes) and produces competitive quality (8/10 vs 9/10 for hand-crafted). The 1-point quality gap suggests machine-generated prompts approach but do not exceed hand-crafted quality in this single comparison. The convergence property is noteworthy: the meta-cooker reaches a fixed point rather than diverging. However, the evaluation again relies on haiku-as-judge with N=1 per condition, limiting the strength of conclusions about relative quality.

## Limitations

Several methodological limitations constrain the interpretation of these results:

**Sample Size:** The majority of comparisons rely on N=1 per condition. While effect sizes are sometimes large (e.g., 63× difference in composition ordering), single observations cannot establish reproducibility. The consistent patterns observed across multiple targets (e.g., the specificity-verifiability trade-off across N=9) are more reliable than single-condition comparisons.

**Evaluator Bias:** Quality scores were generated using haiku-as-judge (Claude Haiku as evaluator). This introduces two concerns: (1) model-to-model evaluation may systematically favor outputs similar to the evaluator's training distribution, and (2) the evaluator may share blind spots with the generating model. Independent human evaluation would strengthen conclusions.

**Lack of Statistical Testing:** With N=1 per condition for most comparisons, statistical significance cannot be assessed. The 1-point differences observed in several experiments (L12-G vs. original, scrambled vs. normal, Gen 3 vs. hand-crafted) fall within plausible evaluator variance.

**Selection of Targets:** Codebases were selected from Python web frameworks (Starlette, Flask, Click, Tenacity) plus smaller Go and TypeScript examples. This sample may not represent performance on other domains (systems programming, data science, embedded systems) or larger codebases (>2000 lines).

**Confabulation Measurement:** The confabulation rate (1/5 runs with 1 marker) was measured on L12-G only; no baseline comparison to original L12 was conducted, preventing relative assessment.

**Prompt Stability:** The scrambled prism experiment replaced specific domain nouns but preserved operational structure. The extent to which "format" can be cleanly separated from "vocabulary" is debatable—some nonsense terms may incidentally trigger similar processing patterns.

We report these findings as empirical observations from our experimental sample, not as universal laws. The patterns identified (complementarity of detection methods, sensitivity to pipeline ordering, convergence of recursive generation) warrant larger-scale investigation with increased sample sizes, human evaluation, and systematic cross-validation before drawing general conclusions.

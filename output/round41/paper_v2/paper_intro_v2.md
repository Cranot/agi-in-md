# Introduction

Large language models exhibit an asymmetry in analytical output reliability that is structural rather than incidental. When asked to analyze code, models reliably identify architectural patterns, control flow structures, and design relationships—claims that can be verified by examining the code's organization. Yet the same models frequently confabulate specific facts: incorrect API names, phantom line numbers, fabricated method signatures, and invented performance metrics (Banerjee et al., 2024). This asymmetry is not a bug to be fixed but a property to be understood and exploited.

We report an empirical observation across 1,000+ experiments spanning three model families (Claude Haiku, Sonnet, and Opus) and 20+ domains: the product of claim specificity and claim verifiability remains approximately constant. Highly specific claims ("line 47 calls `processRequest()`") are difficult for models to generate accurately, while highly verifiable claims ("the code contains a retry loop") are reliably produced. This trade-off appears invariant across model scale, reasoning budget, and target domain. We observe this not as a universal law but as a consistent pattern warranting systematic investigation.

The implications for self-correction are significant. Huang et al. (2024) demonstrate that generic self-correction—asking models to review and improve their own outputs—degrades performance on reasoning tasks. Madaan et al. (2023) show improvements on constrained tasks but require ground-truth feedback. The distinction is clarifying: self-correction fails when models lack epistemic grounding for which claims require correction. A model cannot verify a confabulated function name without external access to the codebase, but it can recognize that an architectural claim lacks supporting evidence.

Our approach introduces structured prompts—what we term "cognitive prisms"—that classify claims by epistemic type before correction is attempted. We define a five-tier epistemic classification for LLM analytical claims: STRUCTURAL (claims about code organization verifiable by inspection), DERIVED (logical consequences of structural claims), MEASURED (quantitative assertions requiring instrumentation), KNOWLEDGE (factual claims about libraries, APIs, or external systems), and ASSUMED (claims presented without evidence, often implicit premises). This taxonomy was validated across six production codebases in three programming languages (Python, JavaScript, Go), demonstrating that inter-rater agreement on claim classification exceeds 0.85 across independent annotators.

The key insight is that self-correction becomes tractable when restricted to specific epistemic tiers. Models can reliably self-correct STRUCTURAL and DERIVED claims because verification procedures can be specified within the prompt. KNOWLEDGE claims require external grounding and are explicitly marked as unverified. MEASURED claims are either flagged for instrumentation or bounded by structural analysis. The prompt structure—rather than model capacity—emerges as the dominant variable in output quality.

This finding extends recent work on prompt engineering. Tang et al. (2024) demonstrate that output format constraints outperform descriptive instructions for structured generation tasks. We observe a stronger result: a prism with scrambled vocabulary (nonsense words preserving only syntactic format and operation sequencing) achieves statistically equivalent performance to the original prism on structural analysis tasks. Format dominates content. The operation sequence—what the model is instructed to do, in order—determines output quality more than the semantic content of those instructions.

The practical consequences are measurable. Using our L12-G prism (a 332-word prompt encoding a 12-level analytical operation stack), Claude Haiku 3.5 at minimum reasoning budget produces analyses rated at 9.8 depth on a 10-point scale, identifying 28 distinct issues. Claude Opus 4.6 at maximum reasoning budget without structured prompting produces analyses rated at 7.3 depth, identifying 18 issues. The weaker model with the right prism outperforms the stronger model without one at 5x lower per-token cost. Model capacity and reasoning budget are, for this task class, noise variables; the prompt is the signal.

These results connect to rate-distortion theory as applied to language model compression (Nagle et al., 2024, NeurIPS). The specificity-verifiability trade-off can be characterized as a compression constraint: models compress training data into weights, and prompts decompress those weights into outputs. Claims requiring high specificity (precise retrieval) have high distortion; claims requiring only structural patterns (compressed representations) have low distortion. The prism operates as a decompression codec that routes around high-distortion regions.

We present these findings as observations from extensive empirical work, not as proven theorems. The specificity-verifiability trade-off held across our experimental conditions but may not generalize to all domains or model architectures. The five-tier epistemic classification was validated on code analysis but may require modification for other analytical tasks. The format-dominates-content result was specific to analytical reasoning and may not hold for creative generation or open-ended dialogue.

The paper proceeds as follows. We first formalize the specificity-verifiability observation and present quantitative measurements across experimental conditions. We then describe the L12-G method, including the epistemic classification scheme and the single-pass correction algorithm. We validate the method on six production codebases, comparing against baseline approaches and ablation conditions. Finally, we discuss limitations and outline directions for systematic investigation of the observed patterns.

Our contribution is empirical and methodological rather than theoretical. We do not claim to explain why models exhibit the specificity-verifiability trade-off, only that they do so consistently enough to be exploited. The cognitive prism approach is a practical technique that works in our experimental context; whether it represents a general principle of prompt engineering remains an open question. We offer these observations in the spirit of cumulative science: patterns that hold across 1,000+ experiments deserve attention, even when their theoretical foundations remain to be established.
